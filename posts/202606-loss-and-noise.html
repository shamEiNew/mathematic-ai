Uncertainty and Loss Functions in Machine Learning

June 16, 2026 • Sham

If you've spent any time training machine learning models, you've probably noticed that the real world refuses to cooperate with our clean mathematical equations. Data is noisy, measurements are imperfect, and there is always a gap between what a model predicts and what actually happens.

That gap is driven by uncertainty. Understanding the nature of uncertainty is one of the most important ideas in machine learning because our assumptions about uncertainty directly determine the loss functions we use every day.

The next time you write loss="mse" or loss="mae", remember that you're making a statistical statement about how you believe the world generates data.

Two Types of Uncertainty

Not all uncertainty is the same. Most prediction errors can be decomposed into two fundamentally different sources:

  • Epistemic Uncertainty (Model Uncertainty)
  • Aleatoric Uncertainty (Data Uncertainty)

1. Epistemic Uncertainty

Epistemic uncertainty comes from a lack of knowledge. It represents things our model does not know yet but could potentially learn if we provided better features, more training examples, or a more expressive model.

Imagine observing only a handful of data points. A straight line may appear to explain everything perfectly. However, after collecting thousands of additional observations, a clear nonlinear trend might emerge.

The underlying relationship was never linear. Our model simply lacked enough information to discover the true structure.

Because this uncertainty can be reduced by gathering additional evidence, it is commonly called model uncertainty.

2. Aleatoric Uncertainty

Aleatoric uncertainty is fundamentally different. It represents randomness that cannot be eliminated, regardless of how much data we collect.

Consider a digital temperature sensor. Even when the room temperature remains fixed at 22°C, repeated measurements might fluctuate slightly:

  • 22.01°C
  • 21.98°C
  • 22.03°C

These fluctuations arise from unavoidable physical noise in the measurement process.

Since this uncertainty is inherent to the data itself, it is often called data uncertainty.

We typically represent this irreducible noise with the symbol $\epsilon$.

From Noise to Probability

When training a model, our true goal is not simply to predict a single value. We are implicitly trying to learn a conditional probability distribution:

$$p(y \mid x)$$

We model the target variable as a combination of a deterministic prediction and random noise:

$$y = f(x;\theta) + \epsilon$$

Here:

  • $f(x;\theta)$ is the model prediction.
  • $\theta$ represents the learnable parameters.
  • $\epsilon$ captures aleatoric noise.

To estimate the optimal parameters $\theta$, we often use Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE).

MLE seeks parameters that maximize the probability of observing the training data.

Instead of maximizing likelihood directly, we minimize the equivalent Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL):

$$\text{NLL}(\theta) = -\sum_{i=1}^{N} \log p(y_i \mid x_i;\theta)$$

This is where loss functions emerge naturally. The exact form of the NLL depends entirely on the probability distribution we assume for the noise term $\epsilon$.

Case 1: Gaussian Noise and Mean Squared Error

The most common assumption is that measurement errors follow a Gaussian (Normal) distribution:

$$\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$$

Under this assumption, the target variable is also normally distributed around the model prediction:

$$y|x;\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(f(x;\theta),\sigma^2)$$

Substituting the Gaussian density into the Negative Log-Likelihood and removing constants that do not depend on $\theta$ yields:

$$\hat{\theta}_{MLE} = \arg\min_{\theta} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i-f(x_i;\theta))^2$$

This is exactly the objective minimized by Mean Squared Error (MSE).

By choosing MSE, you are implicitly assuming that the underlying noise in your data follows a Gaussian distribution.

Because errors are squared, large deviations receive disproportionately large penalties. This makes MSE highly sensitive to outliers.

Case 2: Laplace Noise and Mean Absolute Error

Real-world datasets often contain spikes, anomalies, and heavy-tailed behavior that are poorly described by a Gaussian distribution.

In these situations, a Laplace distribution may provide a better model for the noise:

$$\epsilon \sim \text{Laplace}(0,b)$$

Compared to a Gaussian distribution, a Laplace distribution has:

  • A sharper peak around zero.
  • Significantly heavier tails.

Heavy tails imply that large deviations occur more often than a Gaussian model would predict.

Shifting the distribution to center around our prediction gives:

$$y|x;\theta \sim \text{Laplace}(f(x;\theta),b)$$

Performing the same Negative Log-Likelihood derivation leads to:

$$\hat{\theta}_{MLE} = \arg\min_{\theta} \sum_{i=1}^{N} |y_i-f(x_i;\theta)|$$

This is precisely the objective minimized by Mean Absolute Error (MAE).

Unlike MSE, MAE grows linearly with the size of the error. A single extreme outlier therefore cannot dominate the optimization process.

This robustness is the reason MAE is frequently chosen for datasets with heavy-tailed noise or occasional anomalies.

Summary: The Architecture of Loss

Loss functions are not arbitrary engineering choices. They emerge naturally from probabilistic assumptions about how data is generated.

  • MSE assumes Gaussian noise and strongly penalizes large errors.
  • MAE assumes Laplace noise and is more robust to outliers.

In both cases, the loss function is simply the Negative Log-Likelihood of a particular probabilistic model.

The next time you type loss="mse" or loss="mae", remember that you're not merely selecting an error metric.

You're making a deep statistical claim about the nature of uncertainty in the world you are trying to model.
